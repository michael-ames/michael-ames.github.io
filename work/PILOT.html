I want to be frustrated, I want to be confused


Designing accessible interactive rich experiences for blind and visually-impaired users

Successful experience design tends to avoid negative emotions.

  • Don't make me think

  • Pleasure & amusement

But meaningful, enjoyable human experiences can include all sorts of emotions we might otherwise consider negative

Horror films


Letting yourself get lost in a foreign city


Struggling to finish a jigsaw puzzle

We call these rich experiences

The Rich Experience Framework

  • Negative Stimulus

    A user is exposed to something in the interaction which normally elicits a negative emotion. Their perception of their environment is changed.

  • Protective Frame

    The user is granted the ability to re-frame the situation, to re-evaluate the threat.

  • Transformation of Experience

    What had been an unpleasant or threatening experience is given new meaning and can be enjoyed by the user.

We're going to examine two such rich experiences:
mystery and challenge.

And we're going to look at them in the context of a science museum.

Science museums aren't really about acquiring knowledge

Mystery (i-type)

Curiosity leads to exploration

  • Source emotion:Interest/curiosity
  • Elicited by:
    Novel stimuli; complexity; unusual sensations
  • Behavior:
    Individuals are excited to explore further and discover new features and functions — provided the experience isn't too overwhelming

Mystery (d-type)

Confusion leads to exploration

  • Source emotion:Confusion
  • Elicited by:
    Violated expectations, no prior models, missing information
  • Behavior:
    Individuals desire understanding and are motivated to explore further for answers — provided they believe they can ultimately figure things out

Challenge

Frustration leads to determination

  • Source emotion:Frustration
  • Elicited by:
    Difficult tasks, obstacles, competition, time limits
  • Behavior:
    Individuals want to feel competent and persist in tasks to demonstrate their skill — as long as they feel the context is fair and optional

It's not merely exposure to a negative or uncomfortable stimulus.

The user must actually experience disequilibrium...

And then get back to their "home base."

Museum exhibit designers
are really good at this

Where design strategies for rich experiences fail us

  • Visual-tactile incongruity

  • Size, color, complex visual patterns

  • Visual delivery of feedback

  • Observation and co-participation

The problem with accessibility workarounds

  • Description ≠ Experience

  • Visitors aren't cyborgs

  • Shared, not segregated

So what does this leave us with?

  • Design strategies for rich experience that don't lead to accessible exhibits

  • Accessibility "solutions" that either fail to produce rich experiences, stigmatize the user, or both

What we want

  • Strategies to help designers create exhibits that deliver rich experiences, accessible from the start

  • Respect for the user's autonomy, support inclusion and co-participation

  • Not relying on adding technology to the individual

How you can help

  • Are these new strategies clear?

  • Are they the right size?

  • Which ones are worth validating?
